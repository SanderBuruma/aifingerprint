I've been making the same bolognese for probably fifteen years now and I'm only just realizing I've been doing it wrong the whole time.

Not wrong wrong. It tasted fine. Good, even. But I was browning the meat first, which is what every recipe tells you to do, and it turns out that's the exact opposite of what you want for a proper ragu.

Marcella Hazan — and look, I know everyone cites Marcella Hazan, it's become a cliche at this point, but she's right — she has you cook the meat gently in the soffritto liquid so it never actually browns. The point is to get the meat to release its flavor into the sauce, not to create a crust on it. You're not making a burger. You're making a sauce.

The first time I tried it her way I nearly panicked. Grey meat sitting in liquid, no Maillard reaction, no sizzle. Every instinct said this is wrong. But then you let it go for three hours and the whole thing just... comes together. The meat basically dissolves into the sauce. You can't tell where the meat ends and the sauce begins.

The other thing I changed was using whole milk. You add it after the wine reduces and before the tomatoes go in. I have no idea why this works. Something about the fat coating the meat? Doesn't matter. It makes the sauce taste rounder, less sharp.

My wife says she can't tell the difference. She's lying. I saw her go back for seconds for the first time in maybe a year.

Three hours of simmering seems like a lot but you're not doing anything for most of it. I usually put it on around 2pm on a Sunday, mess around the house, check on it every 30 minutes or so. By dinner time you've got something that tastes like it took actual skill to make when really you just had the patience to leave a pot alone.
