In this study, we conduct a comprehensive analysis of filamentary structures emerging from the Weibel instability, a key mechanism in collisionless plasma dynamics. Our research addresses the fundamental understanding of filamentation processes that play a vital role in various astrophysical and laboratory plasmas, which has implications for magnetic field generation and energy distribution. We employ both analytical and numerical methodologies to gain insights into the intrinsic properties and evolution of these structures. By utilizing high-resolution particle-in-cell simulations, alongside perturbative analytical techniques, we identify the growth rates and spatial configurations of filaments as influenced by initial plasma conditions and anisotropies in velocity distribution functions. Our results reveal that the saturation levels of filamentary structures are significantly affected by the interplay between thermal pressures and magnetic fields. Furthermore, our findings suggest a critical dependence on the background plasma parameters, which dictate the morphology and longevity of filaments. We demonstrate that as anisotropy intensifies, the system evolves through distinct phases characterized by a rapid magnetic field amplification followed by a non-linear saturation regime. This work enhances the predictive capabilities of plasma behavior under varying anisotropic conditions and sheds light on non-equilibrium dynamics integral to understanding broader astrophysical phenomena. Future work will focus on exploring multi-dimensional interactions and extending this framework to more complex configurations. Our analysis provides a foundational step toward optimizing experimental setups in laboratory environments aimed at examining Weibel-mediated processes with precision.
