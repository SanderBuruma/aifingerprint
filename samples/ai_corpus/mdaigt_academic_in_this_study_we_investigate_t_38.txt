In this study, we investigate the properties of extended Lyman-alpha haloes (LAHs) around high-redshift star-forming galaxies using the Multi Unit Spectroscopic Explorer (MUSE) observations in the Hubble Ultra Deep Field (HUDF). Our analysis involves a comprehensive survey of LAHs associated with galaxies at redshifts ranging from 3 to 6. We employ a sophisticated modeling approach, incorporating both observational data and theoretical frameworks, to characterize the spatial extent and luminosity profiles of these LAHs. Our results reveal that the LAHs are considerably larger than the stellar components of their host galaxies, extending to several tens of kiloparsecs. The luminosity of these haloes exhibits a strong correlation with the star formation rates of the central galaxies, suggesting that intense radiation-driven processes potentially influence the properties of the circumgalactic medium. Furthermore, we identify a diversity in halo morphology, indicative of varying environmental interactions and intrinsic galactic properties influencing LAH structures. By comparing with lower redshift samples, we observe evolutionary trends in the LAH population, implying significant changes in feedback processes across cosmic time. Our findings contribute to a deeper understanding of galaxy formation and evolution, illustrating the significant role played by Lyman-alpha emission in tracing the growth of baryonic structures in the early universe. This work significantly enhances our capability to map the distribution of neutral hydrogen and ionized gas around distant galaxies, providing crucial insights into the mechanisms driving star formation and feedback in different cosmic epochs.
