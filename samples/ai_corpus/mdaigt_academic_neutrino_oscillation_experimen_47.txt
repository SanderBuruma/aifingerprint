Neutrino oscillation experiments play a pivotal role in understanding fundamental symmetries in particle physics, particularly concerning charge-parity (CP) violation. The T2K (Tokai to Kamioka) experiment, a leading long-baseline neutrino oscillation study, operates in a dominant neutrino mode, primarily utilizing muon neutrinos to search for CP violation in the lepton sector. This study aims to elucidate the rationale behind T2K's preference for this operational mode and its implications for advancing our understanding of the matter-antimatter asymmetry in the universe.   Employing a comprehensive analysis of the experimental design, oscillation dynamics, and the sensitivity to CP-violating phase parameters, we demonstrate that the choice of running in the dominant neutrino mode significantly enhances the experiment's ability to detect subtle CP violations, particularly within the framework of the Standard Model and beyond. Key findings reveal that the enhanced precision in measuring neutrino interactions and the resultant oscillation patterns provide a clearer signature of CP violation, necessary for discriminating between the possible values of the CP-violating phase, δ_cp.  The conclusion highlights that the T2K's strategic operational alignment towards neutron dominance not only maximizes the signal-to-noise ratio but also aligns with theorized models predicting CP violation effects, thereby establishing a critical pathway in addressing open questions regarding the universe's evolution and the predominance of matter over antimatter.
