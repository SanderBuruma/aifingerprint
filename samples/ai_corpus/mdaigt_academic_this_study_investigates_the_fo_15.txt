This study investigates the forces governing the three-dimensional propagation and expansion of coronal mass ejections (CMEs) as they travel from the Sun to Earth. The objective was to understand the interplay between solar wind dynamics, magnetic field configurations, and gravitational forces acting on CMEs. Utilizing high-resolution solar observatory data and numerical simulations, the research aimed to elucidate the mechanisms responsible for the CME's acceleration, deflection, and subsequent interaction with Earth's magnetosphere.  Methodologically, the study employed both empirical analysis of historical CME events and computational modeling to simulate various scenarios. Key findings revealed that solar wind velocity gradients play a crucial role in determining the initial speed and direction of CMEs. Magnetic reconnection within the CME itself and interactions with pre-existing solar wind streams significantly influence its expansion and morphology. Gravitational forces, particularly those from Earth, were found to cause deviations in the trajectory of CMEs, leading to potential impacts on Earth’s geomagnetic environment.  The conclusion highlights the importance of understanding these forces for predicting CME-induced space weather phenomena. The results underscore the need for improved forecasting models that integrate solar and heliospheric observations with advanced computational techniques to enhance space weather prediction capabilities.
