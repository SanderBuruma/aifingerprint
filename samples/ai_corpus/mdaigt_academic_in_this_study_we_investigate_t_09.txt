In this study, we investigate the implications of a high-mass Higgs scalar within the context of the Minimal Supersymmetric Standard Model (MSSM) on current and future SUSY searches at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). Our analysis focuses on the production channels involving gluons, squarks, and sleptons, as well as the decay modes that would lead to distinctive signatures at the detector. We employ state-of-the-art collider event simulation tools to predict signal cross-sections and backgrounds for various SUSY scenarios. The results indicate that a Higgs boson with a mass greater than 130 GeV could be detected with a significance of about 4-5 standard deviations at the 14 TeV LHC running at design luminosity. This discovery potential is further enhanced by the upcoming High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC) with its increased collision rate, potentially leading to a discovery at around 6-7 standard deviations. Our findings highlight the critical role of precise theoretical predictions and detailed experimental analyses in maximizing the sensitivity of SUSY searches at high-energy colliders. Key methodologies include Monte Carlo simulations, likelihood ratio tests, and Bayesian inference techniques to evaluate the compatibility between model predictions and experimental data.
