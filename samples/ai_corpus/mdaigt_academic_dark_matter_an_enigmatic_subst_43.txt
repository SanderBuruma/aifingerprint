Dark matter, an enigmatic substance accounting for approximately 27% of the total mass-energy content of the universe, has remained elusive due to its lack of electromagnetic interactions. The conventional understanding of dark matter models includes the existence of a hidden sector. However, recent theoretical developments propose the possibility of exciting dark matter directly via its interactions with the Standard Model particles. One such model, the "dark dipole" model, predicts the emission of a 3.55 keV line from WIMP (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles) annihilation or decay. In this study, we present a comprehensive analysis of the possible origin of this line using X-ray observations and astrophysical simulations. We examine the spectral features and spatial distributions of the line in various astrophysical environments, including the Galactic halo, the intracluster medium of galaxy clusters, and active galactic nuclei. Our findings suggest that the 3.55 keV line could originate from radiative decay of excited WIMPs, which do not require a hidden sector. We also discuss potential experimental tests of this model and its implications for the interpretation of observed astrophysical phenomena. This research provides new insights into the nature of dark matter and challenges the conventional assumptions of dark matter models.
