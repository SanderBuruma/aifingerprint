In this study, we address the optimization of treatment plans for stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) by minimizing the number of beams required to achieve an epsilon-optimal solution. The challenge of reducing beam quantity while maintaining treatment efficacy is critical, as it can decrease the patient’s exposure to radiation and improve the overall efficiency of the therapy. We employ a novel optimization framework utilizing a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) approach, coupled with constraints to ensure that the dose distribution adheres to clinical requirements. By integrating gradient-descent algorithms and epsilon-optimal control theory, we have designed a method that guarantees near-optimal solutions with a specified accuracy level, epsilon.   Our methodology ensures that the tumor receives the prescribed dose while minimizing exposure to surrounding healthy tissues and organs at risk. We introduce a constraint relaxation technique to systematically reduce the number of beams used without compromising the treatment's quality. Experimental results demonstrate significant reductions, achieving an average decrease of 30% in the number of beams compared to traditional treatment plans, while maintaining clinically acceptable dosimetric parameters.  We conclude that implementing our optimization strategy could lead to more efficient and safer radiation therapy protocols, reducing both treatment time and patient risk. This work provides a robust framework for future advancements in SBRT treatment planning, emphasizing the importance of mathematical models in enhancing clinical outcomes. Future research will focus on the application of this methodology to other forms of radiation therapy and further refinement of constraints to enhance versatility and effectiveness.
