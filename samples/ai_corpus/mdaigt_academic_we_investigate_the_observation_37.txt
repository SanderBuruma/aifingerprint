We investigate the observational properties of simulated galaxies in both overdense and average regions of the universe at high redshifts (z = 6-12) to better understand early galaxy formation and evolution. Utilizing state-of-the-art hydrodynamic cosmological simulations, we generate a statistically significant sample of mock galaxy populations, emulating observational conditions through radiative transfer processes. Our study focuses on key properties, including stellar mass, star formation rate, morphology, and luminosity functions, comparing these between overdense and average environments. We find that galaxies in overdense regions tend to exhibit higher star formation rates and more complex morphologies due to increased gravitational interactions. In contrast, galaxies in average density environments show slower growth and simpler structures. Furthermore, the luminosity functions derived from our simulations align with observed data at these epochs, suggesting the robustness of our methodology. The suppression and enhancement mechanisms in different regions influence the assembly history, supporting the theory that environmental factors play a critical role in shaping galaxy properties. These insights extend our understanding of how early galaxies might manifest in upcoming deep-field observations and inform predictions for next-generation telescopes. Our work underscores the importance of considering environmental context when studying the early universe and provides a framework for future research on the interplay between galactic and large-scale structure formation. The insights derived here contribute to elucidating the evolution paths of the earliest galaxies, offering significant implications for cosmological models and observational strategies aimed at unveiling the universe's high-redshift frontier.
